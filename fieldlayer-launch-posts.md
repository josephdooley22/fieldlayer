## Post 2 — Buyer Fit

The best first AI workflow in a service business usually is not “AI answers every customer.”

It is one boring leak:

- new leads not structured
- estimates waiting on the owner
- warm deals going stale
- sold work not scheduled cleanly
- review requests never sent

Find the leak. Draft the workflow. Keep humans approving.

## Post 3 — Estimate Workflow

A practical AI estimate workflow is not:

“let AI price jobs and send quotes unsupervised.”

It is:

new request → structured draft → labor/material/sqft assumptions → calculation notes → owner review → correction or approval.

The owner still owns the decision.
The system removes the bottleneck.

## Post 4 — Stale Pipeline

A home service CRM can look busy and still leak money.

The leak is the silent gap:

estimate sent → no follow-up
lead replies → nobody routes it
job sold → not scheduled
invoice done → no review ask

FieldLayer’s lane is the operating layer that sweeps for those gaps and drafts the next move.

## Post 5 — Human-Reviewed AI POV

My rule for FieldLayer:

AI drafts, calculates, sweeps, summarizes, and flags.

Humans approve, correct, send, schedule, and decide.

That is less flashy than “fully automated business.”

It is also much closer to what a real service company can trust.

## Post 6 — CRM Questions Are Workflow Questions

A lot of home service CRM questions are really workflow questions.

Owners ask for:

- shared calendar
- job notes
- estimate/invoice handoff
- follow-up/nurture
- fewer things living in memory

The tool matters.

But first map where work stalls:

lead → estimate → follow-up → schedule → review

Then install AI as a draft-and-sweep layer around that workflow.
